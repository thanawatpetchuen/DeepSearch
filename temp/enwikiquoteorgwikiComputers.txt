<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Jay David Bolter</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
								<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Jay David Bolter</b> (born August 17, 1951) is the Wesley Chair of New Media and a <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> in the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Literature,_Media,_and_Communication" title="School of Literature, Media, and Communication">School of Literature, Media, and Communication</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Institute of Technology</a>. His areas of study include the evolution of media, the use of technology in education, and the role of computers in the writing process. More recently, he has conducted research in the area of <a href="/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a> and mixed media. Bolter collaborates with researchers in the Augmented Environments Lab, co-directed with Blair MacIntyre, to create apps for entertainment, cultural heritage and education for smart phones and tablets. This supports his theory regarding remediation where he discusses "all media functions as remediators and that remediation offers us a means of interpreting the work of earlier media as well" (Bolter &amp; Grusin, 2000, p.&#160;55).</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jay_David_Bolter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bolter received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Trinity_College" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Trinity College">Trinity College</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>, in 1973. In 1977 and 1978 he received his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classics</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">M.S.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a>, both from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina</a>.</p>
<p>Bolter received prominent fellowships at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg-August_University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg-August University of Göttingen">University of Göttingen</a>, and with the American Council of Learned Societies.</p>
<p>From 1979 until 1991, Bolter held various faculty positions at the University of North Carolina. In 1991 he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Institute_of_Technology" title="Georgia Institute of Technology">Georgia Institute of Technology</a>, where he remains today.<sup id="cite_ref-shortcv_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shortcv-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storyspace">Storyspace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jay_David_Bolter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Storyspace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with John B. Smith and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Joyce_(writer)" title="Michael Joyce (writer)">Michael Joyce</a>, Bolter co-created <a href="/wiki/Storyspace" title="Storyspace">Storyspace</a>, a software program for creating, editing, and displaying <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_fiction" title="Hypertext fiction">hypertext fiction</a>. It was developed to support hypertext fiction in particular, although it can also be used for organizing and writing fiction and non-fiction intended for print.<sup id="cite_ref-shortcv_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shortcv-1">[1]</a></sup> Although always credited as a coauthor of Storyspace with Joyce and Bolter, Smith wanted to clarify in an interview that he wasn’t involved in the development of either TALETELLER (which was a precursor to Storyspace) or Storyspace — but that he made more of an intellectual contribution insofar as "there was a sort of cloud of ideas that we were all drawing on in the discussions we’d have in this research entity at UNC, Textlab" <sup id="cite_ref-barnet-what_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnet-what-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the notable hypertext fictions created in Storyspace include: <a href="/wiki/Michael_Joyce_(writer)" title="Michael Joyce (writer)">Michael Joyce</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Afternoon,_a_story" title="Afternoon, a story">afternoon, a story</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Moulthrop" title="Stuart Moulthrop">Stuart Moulthrop</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Victory_Garden_(novel)" title="Victory Garden (novel)">Victory Garden</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Shelley_Jackson" title="Shelley Jackson">Shelley Jackson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Patchwork_Girl_(hypertext)" title="Patchwork Girl (hypertext)">Patchwork Girl</a></i>.</p>
<p>Bolter has used <a href="/wiki/Storyspace" title="Storyspace">Storyspace</a> to revise several of his own books. More importantly, Storyspace provides facilities for writing and editing, which includes a map of the structure of the links, making it accessible for new users. Storyspace is currently being developed by Mark Bernstein of <a href="/wiki/Eastgate_Systems" title="Eastgate Systems">Eastgate Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hayles-what_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hayles-what-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jay_David_Bolter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">Brian Eno</a> has referred to Bolter as "the new <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Gutenberg</a>." <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bolter states in <i>Writing Space: Computers, Hypertext, and the Remediation of Print</i> that "Hypertext in all its electronic forms — the World Wide Web as well as the many stand-alone systems — is the remediation of print".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jay David Bolter and his writing partner, Richard Grusin, make the claim in their text <i>Remediation: Understanding New Media, "</i> At this point, all mediation is remediation."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
